Claim denials are a reality in healthcare billing, but they do not all require the same follow-up approach. Different denial reasons call for different workflows, documentation, and appeal strategies.

## Most common denial categories

**Eligibility and authorization denials** occur when the patient's coverage was not verified before the service, or when required prior authorization was not obtained. These are typically preventable with better front-end processes.

**Coding and billing denials** result from incorrect CPT, ICD-10, or HCPCS codes; missing modifiers; unbundling errors; or mismatched diagnosis and procedure codes. These often require corrected claims rather than appeals.

**Medical necessity denials** occur when the payer determines that the service was not medically necessary for the patient's condition. These typically require a formal appeal with supporting clinical documentation.

**Timely filing denials** happen when claims are not submitted within the payer's specified filing window. These are generally not appealable but may have exceptions in specific circumstances.

**Duplicate claim denials** occur when a claim has already been processed. These require research to determine whether it was a true duplicate or a separate service that needs to be resubmitted with corrections.

## Structured follow-up workflow

1. Categorize each denial by reason code
2. Determine the appropriate response (corrected claim, appeal, or write-off)
3. Verify timely filing and appeal deadlines for the specific payer
4. Prepare required documentation or corrected claim
5. Submit and track the response
6. Follow up if no response is received within the expected timeframe
7. Document the outcome for trend analysis

## Preventing future denials

Tracking denial reasons over time reveals patterns. Common root causes include front-end data errors, coding gaps, authorization lapses, and claim submission errors. Addressing these at the process level reduces future denial volume.
